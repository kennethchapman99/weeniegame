# Co-op Puzzle Design Doctrine

This document defines the co-op magic standard for Cheddar & Cocoa. It exists because a mission can be playable, tested, and readable while still feeling too straightforward. Future levels should feel closer to premium co-op adventure design: the players laugh because they need each other in a specific, surprising, dog-authentic way.

## Definition

A Cheddar & Cocoa puzzle is not a slow brainteaser. It is a short playable dependency where:

1. one dog can see, reach, trigger, hold, smell, distract, or survive something the other cannot;
2. the other dog must interpret that opening and do a different action;
3. the solution creates a visible world change;
4. failure is funny, recoverable, and informative;
5. the beat takes 10-60 seconds, not 5 minutes of confusion.

The standard is: **I can do my dog thing, but only you can turn it into progress.**

## Design promise

Every substantial mission should include at least one authored co-op puzzle beat, not just shared collection, shared survival, or simultaneous standing in a circle. The beat may be tiny in early prototypes, but the design intent must be present.

A mission should not pass design review if both players can independently do the same task in parallel until a counter reaches zero. That is an activity, not co-op magic.

## The co-op puzzle ladder

Use this ladder when expanding a mission. Higher rungs are better, but not every beat needs all of them.

1. **Shared target** - both dogs work on the same object, e.g. tug the rope together.
2. **Split roles** - one dog holds/blocks/distracts while the other completes the action.
3. **Split information** - one dog sees/smells/hears the clue; the other has the tool or position to act on it.
4. **Sequential cause/effect** - Dog A changes the world so Dog B can reach/trigger something new.
5. **Role reversal** - after the first success, the game flips who has the enabling role.
6. **Comic complication** - the obvious solution creates a dog-life problem: leash tangles, Cheddar takes bait, Cocoa refuses indignity, human notices, toy rolls away.
7. **Readable deception** - trick a squirrel, human, robot, coyote, vacuum, or eagle using dog behavior.
8. **Dual timing** - both players act at different times, not merely at the same time.
9. **Choice under pressure** - players decide who risks panic, water, line-of-sight, snack temptation, leash tension, or human attention.
10. **Punchline payoff** - success triggers a funny, personal, memorable result.

## Puzzle families to reuse

### 1. Hold-and-release

One dog holds a pressure condition while the other acts.

Dog-authentic versions:
- Cocoa anchors a pool noodle, blanket, leash, or couch cushion while Cheddar crosses, jumps, steals, or squeezes through.
- Cheddar keeps a squirrel yelling at him while Cocoa sneaks behind it.
- One dog sits on a floor vent, towel, or pressure mat while the other rides/uses the effect.

Good failure:
- The held object snaps back, flings a toy, or lightly bonks a dog.

### 2. Distract-and-sneak

One dog pulls attention while the other moves unseen.

Dog-authentic versions:
- Bark at a human, vacuum, delivery driver, coyote, robot, or giant dog to rotate its attention cone.
- Cocoa uses royal staring/sitting to hold a human's gaze while Cheddar steals a sock.
- Cheddar makes chaos so Cocoa can perform the clean objective.

Good failure:
- The distracted actor turns around, the sneaking dog freezes, drops the item, or gets gently escorted away.

### 3. Smell-and-act

One dog reads the invisible clue; the other can manipulate the object.

Dog-authentic versions:
- Cheddar smells which grocery bag has food; Cocoa opens the right route.
- Cocoa smells which blanket/toy belongs to the desired human; Cheddar can drag it.
- One dog sees hot/cold scent feedback while the other is near the dig/push/carry object.

Good failure:
- Wrong scent causes sneeze, laundry confusion, or a useless decoy toy.

### 4. Bait-and-switch

The dogs intentionally manipulate an enemy or human into choosing the wrong target.

Dog-authentic versions:
- Cheddar pretends to chase a squirrel toward Cocoa's ambush.
- Cocoa holds composure near a fake snack while Cheddar must be physically steered away.
- One dog drags a decoy toy/noisy object to pull a vacuum, coyote, eagle shadow, or teenager away.

Good failure:
- The bait works too well and creates a new mess.

### 5. Physical bridge / long-dog geometry

Use dachshund bodies, leashes, blankets, ramps, furniture, and toys as spatial tools.

Dog-authentic versions:
- Long-dog bridge over a small gap.
- Leash wraps around a chair leg to pull open a route.
- Dogs stretch a blanket between them to catch falling food or shield from thunder.
- One dog wedges under a gate while the other squeezes through.

Good failure:
- The bridge dog runs out of patience and flops.

### 6. Split-screen-equivalent information without split screen

The camera remains shared, but the game can still separate knowledge.

Dog-authentic versions:
- Only the dog near a smell trail gets directional scent puffs.
- One dog hears/feels thunder timing first and must lead the huddle.
- One dog is inside a box/blanket/cabinet and sees internal arrows while the other sees the external hazard.

Good failure:
- Players can still recover by reading stronger pings, arrows, and barks.

### 7. Timed double action

Not just "press together". Dog A starts a window; Dog B finishes inside it.

Dog-authentic versions:
- Cheddar barks to make a human drop food; Cocoa must catch before it hits forbidden ground.
- Cocoa steadies an object; Cheddar has a short zoomie window to cross.
- One dog rattles a doorstop; the other nudges the door while it is vibrating.

Good failure:
- The opportunity resets quickly with a funny animation.

### 8. Rescue-as-puzzle

Rescue should sometimes require more than proximity barking.

Dog-authentic versions:
- A stuck dog can only wiggle/whine/lean to aim themselves while the free dog pulls, distracts, or changes the environment.
- The held dog can reveal timing or weaken a grip; the free dog chooses the correct rescue action.
- Rescue first creates a new problem: wet dog, tangled leash, dropped object, startled human.

Good failure:
- The stuck dog gets more dramatic, not permanently punished.

### 9. Social manipulation

Humans are puzzle systems, not only hazards.

Dog-authentic versions:
- Trigger a pee break by combining leash, shoe, door stare, bark rhythm, and blocked hallway.
- Move a strict human away from a table while a soft-hearted human drops snacks.
- Make a teenager look up by unplugging charger, squeaking toy, and synchronized pathetic stare.

Good failure:
- Human misunderstands and gives the wrong thing: toy instead of walk, blanket instead of food, bath instead of rescue.

### 10. Cooperative chaos machines

Build contraptions where players understand the pieces but the dog execution is messy.

Dog-authentic versions:
- Drag towel -> knock basket -> reveal toy -> trigger vacuum -> open route.
- Tug blanket -> launch cushion -> expose sock -> steal sock before human retrieves it.
- Push pool noodle -> bridge floaty -> rescue toy -> shake water onto floor -> slip-slide escape.

Good failure:
- The machine misfires, but players see which step failed.

## Cheddar/Cocoa role language

Use identity to make puzzles feel personal instead of generic.

Cheddar should often be:
- faster initiator;
- chaos trigger;
- food/squirrel magnet;
- risky jumper/squeezer;
- decoy bait;
- panic amplifier;
- comic failure engine.

Cocoa should often be:
- anchor;
- territory holder;
- calmer/rescuer;
- steady timing partner;
- social stare specialist;
- better at patience/precision;
- dignity-based contrast to Cheddar's chaos.

Do not hard-lock every puzzle by dog unless it creates comedy or clarity. Prefer soft asymmetry first: both can try, but one is better, safer, faster, or funnier.

## Mission structure template

A strong mission should have this shape:

1. **Dog fantasy hook** - why the dogs care.
2. **Basic verb intro** - safe first use of the mechanic.
3. **First co-op lock** - one dog cannot progress without the other.
4. **Twist** - role reversal, moving hazard, fake-out, or temptation.
5. **Escalation** - same idea under pressure, not a totally unrelated mechanic.
6. **Punchline finish** - success is funny and visually memorable.
7. **Readable fail path** - the fail teaches the solution and invites replay.

## Design acceptance checklist

Before building or expanding a mission, answer:

- What is the co-op lock?
- What can Dog A do that Dog B cannot do at that moment?
- What can Dog B do because Dog A acted?
- Does the solution require communication, timing, or interpretation?
- Is there a role reversal or twist before the beat gets stale?
- What is the funny failure?
- What is the visible world-state change?
- What is the shortest playable prototype of the beat?
- What can be PlayMode tested deterministically?

## Anti-patterns

Avoid these unless intentionally used as a warm-up:

- Two dogs independently collect objects until a counter fills.
- Both dogs stand in the same circle with no reason other than gating.
- Bark works as a universal answer everywhere.
- The objective label tells the whole solution instead of nudging discovery.
- Failure silently subtracts score without showing what went wrong.
- Cheddar and Cocoa are mechanically identical in every puzzle.
- Puzzle beats last so long that comedy turns into confusion.
- One player waits while the other plays.

## Example upgrades for current/future missions

### Backyard Rescue

Upgrade from collect/protect/tug into a squirrel trap sequence:
- Cheddar barks too close and scares the squirrel into a fake route.
- Cocoa can hold a garden gate/cover gap to redirect it.
- Once redirected, Cheddar steals back the dropped weenie while Cocoa blocks the escape.

### Snack Heist

Make forbidden snacks a stealth puzzle:
- One dog distracts the human/table watcher.
- The other nudges a chair, grabs a snack, and carries it through a safe lane.
- Snack smell attracts Cheddar harder, so Cocoa may need to body-block him from bait.

### Sock Panic

Make sock retrieval a laundry-machine puzzle:
- One dog pulls the laundry basket cloth handle.
- The other dives into the exposed sock pile.
- Wrong sock creates a decoy trail or gets reclaimed by the human.

### Squirrel Conspiracy

Lean harder into tricking the squirrel:
- One dog reveals fake stash clues by sniffing.
- The other silently occupies the real escape route.
- A united bark only works after both dogs have proven the route is blocked.

### Eagle Shadow Panic

Make cover dynamic:
- Cocoa holds a patio umbrella/tablecloth open as temporary shade.
- Cheddar must dash through that shade window to rescue the toy.
- Next sweep reverses roles or moves the cover.

### Coyotes at the Fence

Make repair a three-step role puzzle:
- Bark-pin the coyote.
- Dig/fill dirt while pressure is held.
- Tug a garden hose/board across the repaired gap before the coyote returns.

### Weenie Roundup

Make carrying require teamwork:
- Heavy jumbo weenie needs two dogs to carry slowly.
- Cheddar can solo-carry small ones but is tempted to eat/fumble.
- Cocoa can steady a carried item through narrow gates.

### Scent Search

Make scent information asymmetric:
- Cheddar gets strong food/bone scent but poor direction near laundry.
- Cocoa gets steadier direction but weaker range.
- One dog sniffs from high/clean ground while the other digs.

### Thunderstorm Comfort

Make comfort active:
- One dog gathers blanket/toy comfort objects.
- The other maintains huddle/calm zone.
- Thunder changes which object is needed next.

### Mark the Yard

Make territory claims require setup:
- Some zones need scent priming from one dog and final claim from the other.
- Squirrel can be baited away by leaving a decoy claim.
- Cocoa holds claimed territory longer; Cheddar can rapidly contest stolen zones.

### Walkies on the Leash

Make leash wrapping useful, not only a penalty:
- Wrap around a post to pull open a gate.
- Unwrap before tension annoys the human.
- One dog must resist a smell while the other uses the leash angle.

### Car Ride Balance

Make car balance a communication puzzle:
- One dog sees incoming lurch warning through the window.
- The other must move first to counterweight.
- A snack/toy slides across the seat and tempts the wrong movement.

## Implementation rule

For future Codex goals, each new mission prompt should include a **Co-op Puzzle Beat** section with:

- beat name;
- player roles;
- lock/key relationship;
- readable hints;
- funny fail;
- deterministic test hooks.

If the prompt cannot name the beat, the mission is not ready to build.
